## Problem

My trading review was split across too many places.

<SplitSources
  sources={[
    'Broker CSVs had the numbers.',
    'Journal notes had the psychology.',
    'Whoop had the readiness.',
    'Weekly reflections lived somewhere else.',
  ]}
/>

The information was there, connecting the dots was the hard part.

I could see the pieces individually but making one honest picture of the week meant manual stitching, memory, and guesswork.

The real problem was not a lack of data. It was a lack of one place where the data could challenge the story I was telling myself.

## Goal

Build a single **operator dashboard** for trade review.

The system needed to answer:

<CaseStudyQuestions
  questions={[
    'Did I follow the plan?',
    'Where does my edge actually show up?',
    'Which sessions leak money?',
    'Does readiness affect execution?',
    'What emotional patterns keep repeating?',
    "What should next week's focus be?",
  ]}
/>

The aim was process clarity, not a focus on P&L.

## System

<SystemTimeline
  phases={[
    {
      title: 'Capture',
      steps: [
        'Import trades, sync journal notes, log daily readiness and rule compliance.',
      ],
    },
    {
      title: 'Diagnose',
      steps: [
        'Break the week down by session, setup, instrument, timing, tags, and rule behaviour.',
      ],
    },
    {
      title: 'Synthesize',
      steps: [
        'Generate weekly Performance and Psychology reviews that turn raw trades into a clean operating focus.',

    },
    {

  ]}

## What makes it different

Most trading dashboards ask: "Did you make money?"

Libertrade asks:

<CaseStudyQuestions
  questions={[
    'Did you follow the process?',
    'Was your state clean?',
    'Did you trade the setup or your P&L?',
    'Did low recovery change your execution?',
    'Did you get paid for your best trades?',
    'Which rule needs to become mechanical next week?',
  ]}
/>

<p>That is the difference between a journal and an operating system.</p>
